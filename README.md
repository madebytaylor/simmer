# 🍳 Simmer
### AI-powered dinner decisions for exhausted parents — because 5pm shouldn't require a plan.

**[→ View Live Prototype](https://madebytaylor.github.io/simmer)**

---

## The Problem

Working parents with young kids have a brutal daily window: daycare pickup at 4:30, bedtime at 7:00, and somewhere in between — dinner. After a full workday, the mental energy to decide what to cook, figure out what's in the house, and get something a toddler will actually eat is just gone. The default? Expensive takeout. Again.

Every existing meal planning app assumes you're the kind of person who plans ahead on a calm Sunday afternoon. **Simmer is built for the parent who didn't.**

---

## The Solution

Simmer is a mobile-first app with two core components:

- **Pantry Profile** — Set up once. Tell Simmer your household staples, your toddler's anchor foods (the ones they reliably eat), and your max cook time. Flag this week's extras in under 60 seconds.
- **Tonight** — Tap one button at 5pm. Get 2–3 dinner suggestions for the next few nights, built from what you actually have, in 30 minutes or less, that your kids will eat. Share options with your partner with one tap — no conversation required.

## Prototype Walkthrough

The prototype covers the full primary flow:

1. **Home** — Family overview and quick actions
2. **Generating** — AI finds meals based on your pantry and family preferences
3. **Suggestions** — 3 meal options with cook time, difficulty, and toddler-friendliness ratings
4. **Meal Detail** — Prep steps and a Finn-specific tip for getting a 3-year-old to eat it
5. **Share with Partner** — One-tap async decision-making, no conversation needed
6. **Feedback** — Rate the meal and log whether the toddler ate it to improve future suggestions

Use the dots at the bottom to jump between screens during a demo.

## About This Project

I'm a product manager and a parent of a 3-year-old and an 8-month-old. This project started as a personal pain point and became an end-to-end PM exercise: problem discovery, user research, competitive analysis, product brief, and a no-code interactive prototype.

The goal was to practice the full product development process — from "this is annoying" to "here's a shippable V1 definition" — on a problem I know deeply and care about.
